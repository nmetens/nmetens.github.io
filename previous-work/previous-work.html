August 2021 - September 2021: IT Technical Support Specialist

In late August, I got a call from the French American International School (FAIS)

In late August, I received a call from the French American International School (FAIS), where my mom works, because they needed a new tech support specialist. I didn't know much about tech support, but I was more than willing to give it a try. I was scheduled for a meeting the day after the call, and began working immediatly. I am only filling the position temporarily, for the month of September because classes at Portland State begin towards the end of September. So far, I have been getting hundreds of iPads and Chromebooks ready for the students by charging them, labeling them with ID's and names, and checking them in on an open source software called Snipe-it, which acts as a library to keep track of user technology. I have simultaneously been the helpdesk for all the teachers on campus, helping with printer setup/issues, projectors, computer software problems, and other miscelaneous tech questions. What I love about working in tech support is that I get to show people how to use technology and help them figure out how to fix their queries. I interact with many teachers who speak mostly French to me and that's also very cool. Aside from the main tasks of tech support and technology set-up, I have also helped by cleaning and preparing the tech room for optimum performance, like getting rid of old monitors, keybaords, mice, and the like, donating them to Free Geek, a non-profit that helps reduce e-waste in the US. I am also very curious about how the server on campus works. I am new to computers (I only got a minor in computer science so far) and want to learn everything I can about networks, software, hardware, and security, so I ask a lot of questions to the tech manager. I have been working for about 38 hours each week so far and I find it to be rather fun. I have learned a few things, and I can't wait to begin my masters program at Portland State to learn much more.

In 2016, my sisters and I began helping my parents with the family business. My dad had built his very own waffle/crepe truck from scratch, using a Uhaul truck as his base. His brother had a pizza truck in Belgium, and it was successful, so this inspired my father to bring Belgian pastries to Portland and Beaverton for people to enjoy. My sisters and I would help the business at farmers' markets every weekend by cashiering, decorating the waffles/crepes with toppings, and baking the waffles/crepes. Every year, the popularity of this little business started growing, and my father would get asked if he had a restaurant or a website. He had neither. He began scouting the area for more places to sell the delicious pastries. He had a spot at the OHSU market, the Beaverton Saturday Market, and he often rented space in parking lots during the week. Eventually, we were getting asked to cater at special events, like weddings, back-to-school days, Belgian International parties, birthdays, and the like.

While I was helping my mother and father at a wedding to prepare, decorate, and serve over one hundred people our waffles. It hit me that I needed to create a website for our family business — formally called "Oofty Waffles & Crepes" — when four wedding guests asked my dad for more information about his location, catering, and contact. They had loved the waffles so much and wanted more. Now that I have successfully created and launched my personal website (using HTML, CSS, and JavaScript), I am equipped with the necessary skills to craft a website for our family business. My next project is to do just that.

A couple months ago I was helping my mother and father at a wedding to prepare, decorate, and serve over one hundred people our waffles. Now that I have successfully created and launched my own personal website from scratch (using HTML, CSS, and JavaScript), I am equipped with the necessary skills to craft a starter website for our family business. My next project, to do just that.
